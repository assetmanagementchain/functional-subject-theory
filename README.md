# Functional Subject Theory

[中文版本](README_zh.md)

## Overview

Functional Subject Theory studies how effective subjects of production, exchange, governance, and responsibility may be formed in the AI era.

The theory does not assume that the fundamental operating subjects of society must always be natural persons, companies, governments, or other historically fixed organizations.

Instead, it asks whether a stable functional system can become an effective subject when it can:

- provide a persistent external function;
- maintain an internal decision loop;
- control or coordinate necessary resources;
- form its own cost, preference, and reservation-price structure;
- enter contracts or other coordination relationships;
- preserve evidence of performance;
- receive returns and bear losses;
- accept responsibility through an identifiable liability structure;
- maintain, reorganize, divide, merge, or exit according to changing production conditions.

Such a subject may consist of:

- one natural person;
- part of a natural person’s capabilities;
- multiple natural persons;
- one or more AI systems;
- tools, machines, data, and automated processes;
- components distributed across existing organizations;
- a stable human–AI production system.

The boundary of a functional subject is therefore determined primarily by stable function, decision, resource, exchange, and responsibility structures rather than by biological, administrative, or legal identity alone.

## Core Distinction

Functional Subject Theory separates two different layers:

### Person as a rights and experience subject

A natural person remains the subject of:

- basic rights;
- bodily autonomy;
- lived experience;
- consent;
- refusal;
- appeal;
- personal welfare;
- freedom to choose whether to provide a capability.

### Functional subject as an operating subject

A functional subject may become the subject of:

- production;
- exchange;
- task commitment;
- resource coordination;
- pricing;
- governance;
- evidence accumulation;
- operational responsibility.

This distinction does not eliminate the natural person. It rejects the assumption that one biological person must always correspond to one indivisible economic, organizational, and governance subject.

## Capability Is Not Supply

Having a capability does not mean that a person or functional subject is willing to provide it.

Effective supply depends on conditions such as:

- expected return;
- direct cost;
- opportunity cost;
- time requirement;
- risk;
- learning value;
- strategic value;
- autonomy;
- future benefit;
- non-monetary reward.

Therefore:

Capability ≠ Willingness
Willingness ≠ Transaction

A transaction or coordination relationship becomes possible only when the acceptable contract ranges of the relevant subjects overlap.

Price may be multidimensional and may include:

money;
resource access;
future revenue;
risk allocation;
time flexibility;
learning opportunities;
reputation;
decision rights;
long-term participation rights.
Subject Formation

A functional subject is not created merely by assigning a role or combining several tools.

A functional system may develop into a subject when repeated production creates a sufficiently stable combination of:

Complementary capabilities
+ persistent function
+ internal coordination advantage
+ decision closure
+ resource control
+ external exchange capacity
+ evidence continuity
+ liability structure
= candidate functional subject

Subject boundaries are treated as endogenous and evolutionary.

They may change when:

coordination costs change;
AI reduces search or contracting costs;
capabilities become reproducible;
new tools alter production structure;
responsibility can be reassigned;
a formerly stable system becomes inefficient;
a temporary collaboration develops persistent functional continuity.
Relationship to Existing Mechanisms

Functional Subject Theory is not itself a single allocation or governance mechanism.

It operates at a more basic layer by asking what kinds of entities the mechanisms act upon.

Possible coordination mechanisms include:

markets;
auctions;
contracts;
planning;
platform matching;
community governance;
polycentric governance;
public budgeting;
negotiation;
hybrid mechanisms.

The conceptual order is:

Subject ontology
→ value and price formation
→ allocation and coordination mechanisms
→ governance, auditing, and correction

Markets, mechanism design, capability approaches, transaction-cost economics, and polycentric governance may all be incorporated into the theory without being replaced by it.

Research Questions

The project currently focuses on several unresolved questions:

Under what conditions does a functional system become an independent subject rather than remain a component?
How should subject boundaries be identified?
How are returns, costs, and liabilities distributed among internal components?
How do functional subjects form their own preference and reservation-price structures?
How do subjects merge, divide, evolve, reproduce, or exit?
How can natural-person rights remain protected when production subjects become increasingly non-biological?
How can functional subjects be prevented from developing permanent power, monopoly, or self-preservation structures?
How should legal responsibility pass through AI, human, organizational, and technical components?
Which existing organizations are already evolving toward functional subjects?
Under what conditions are functional subjects more efficient or more legitimate than traditional firms and institutions?

alternative definitions;
empirical evidence;
implementation proposals;
independent extensions.
