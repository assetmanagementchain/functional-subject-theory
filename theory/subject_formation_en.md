## 1. Purpose of This Document

This document studies:

How functional subjects emerge endogenously through long-term social practice, division of labor, repeated collaboration, and competition among multiple subjects.

A functional subject is not automatically created by a name, registration, position, legal form, or single task.

A theory of subject formation must explain:

why certain capabilities, resources, rules, and liability relationships repeatedly combine;
why some combinations exist only temporarily for a single task;
why some combinations gradually form stable internal closure;
why some subjects develop highly specialized capabilities for narrow goals;
why others develop robust and general-purpose capabilities across goals and environments;
why some subjects persist over time while others divide, are absorbed, or disappear;
how AI changes the cost conditions of subject formation, maintenance, and recomposition;
why subject stability does not automatically imply social optimality or legitimacy.

Functional-subject formation should be understood as:

an endogenous process;
a historical process;
a cost-compression process;
a competitive-selection process;
a dynamic-equilibrium process;
a reversible evolutionary process.

## 2. Core Proposition

Functional Subject Theory proposes the following core proposition:

A functional subject is a stable functional structure that gradually forms through repeated combinations of capabilities, resources, knowledge, rules, machines, natural persons, AI systems, and liability relationships in long-term social practice and division of labor, usually under pressure to reduce the total social cost of realizing a class of goals.

Through repeated practice, this structure gradually internalizes external dependencies that are frequent, costly, or critical, and develops:

decision closure;
learning closure;
resource-coordination closure;
evidence closure;
exchange closure;
return-and-loss-bearing structures;
liability closure.

After formation, a subject may appear as:

a temporary subject;
a stable subject;
a goal-specific subject;
a robust general-purpose subject;
a highly externally dependent subject;
a highly internally closed subject;
an easily replaceable subject;
a subject stable across multiple environments and competitive conditions.

Whether a subject forms, persists, expands, divides, or disappears depends on the interaction of cost, adaptation, competition, institutions, path dependence, and power.

## 3. Subject Formation Is Not the Granting of Status

A functional subject does not become a subject merely because it satisfies a static checklist and is then granted subject status by an external authority.

A more accurate process is:

Social goals repeatedly appear
→ different components attempt combinations
→ combinations perform tasks and expose real costs
→ effective relationships are repeatedly reused
→ high-frequency relationships form stable interfaces
→ tacit knowledge and coordination experience accumulate
→ some external dependencies become internalized
→ internal closure gradually forms
→ stable external function and decision capacity emerge
→ the structure is tested against other subjects and substitute combinations
→ temporary or long-term subject stability forms

Closure is therefore not necessarily a complete prior structure that must exist before subject formation.

Closure itself is a historical structure produced by long-term practice and cost compression.

## 4. Total Social Cost of Functional Realization

One major force behind subject formation is the reduction of the total cost required to realize a class of social goals.

Cost here does not mean monetary expenditure alone.

The total social cost of functional realization may include:

direct production cost;
search cost;
capability-identification cost;
component-matching cost;
contracting cost;
communication cost;
decision cost;
coordination cost;
interface-adaptation cost;
learning cost;
knowledge-transfer cost;
verification cost;
trust-building cost;
risk-identification cost;
liability-identification cost;
supervision cost;
failure cost;
reconstruction cost after failure;
subject-maintenance cost;
subject-exit cost;
externality cost.

For a candidate structure A, goal g, and environment e, the total social cost may be represented conceptually as:

C_total(A, g, e) = C_production + C_search + C_matching + C_coordination + C_decision + C_adaptation + C_verification + C_liability + C_maintenance + C_failure + C_externality

Where:

A: candidate subject structure;
g: goal;
e: environment;
C_production: direct production cost;
C_search: search cost;
C_matching: matching cost;
C_coordination: coordination cost;
C_decision: decision cost;
C_adaptation: adaptation cost;
C_verification: verification cost;
C_liability: liability-identification and liability-bearing cost;
C_maintenance: subject-maintenance cost;
C_failure: failure and reconstruction cost;
C_externality: externality cost.

Subject formation often occurs when:

The long-term total cost of preserving a set of capabilities and relationships as a stable structure is lower than the long-term total cost of repeatedly searching, verifying, combining, and coordinating those capabilities for each new goal.

This can be represented as:

C_stable(A) < C_recompose(A)

Where:

C_stable(A): the long-term cost of maintaining candidate structure A as a stable subject;
C_recompose(A): the long-term cost of repeatedly searching, verifying, combining, and coordinating components.

However, real societies do not necessarily produce a globally optimal subject structure.

More often, several locally stable structures emerge under limited information, historical constraints, path dependence, and competition.

## 5. Cost Compression Is Not the Only Driver

Subject formation is often driven by cost compression, but cost compression cannot be treated as the only force.

Subject formation and persistence may also be influenced by:

concentration of power;
coercion;
legal protection;
resource monopoly;
network effects;
path dependence;
historical routines;
cultural identity;
social identity;
military pressure;
political pressure;
restrictions on exit;
information control;
standard lock-in;
initial advantage;
sunk costs.

Therefore:

Stable subject ≠ Lowest-social-cost subject

Stable subject ≠ Socially optimal subject

Stable subject ≠ Legitimate subject

Some subjects remain stable not because they minimize total social cost, but because they control resources, rules, information, or channels of replacement.

Functional Subject Theory must therefore separately analyze:

functional effectiveness;
cost structure;
competitive survival;
power structure;
institutional protection;
social legitimacy.

## 6. Starting Points of Subject Formation

Subject formation usually begins with some combination of:

a goal;
a set of repeated demands;
capability fragments;
natural persons;
one or more AI systems;
tools;
machines;
data;
knowledge;
rules;
resources;
liability requirements;
temporary collaboration relationships;
partial modules within existing organizations.

At first, these objects form a functional combination rather than a stable functional subject.

Examples include:

a designer temporarily using an image model;
several freelancers completing one project;
a group of AI agents temporarily generating software;
a physician using a diagnosis model for one judgment;
two firms jointly completing one order;
a public agency creating a temporary emergency team.

These combinations may successfully complete tasks, but one successful task does not prove that a subject has formed.

## 7. Component Combination and Structural Variation

For the same class of goals, society may produce many different component combinations.

For example, the same software-development goal may be performed by:

one natural person;
a fixed development team;
one natural person working with several AI agents;
temporary collaboration among several firms;
a platform-based combination of freelancers;
a highly automated production system.

These combinations expose different levels of:

cost;
quality;
speed;
risk;
liability structure;
learning capacity;
robustness;
recomposition difficulty.

Different combinations can be understood as structural variations in the process of subject formation.

A subject does not emerge from one predetermined design. It emerges through repeated attempts, competition, selection, preservation, and recomposition.

## 8. Repeated Practice and Relationship Retention

When similar goals repeatedly appear, some relationships within successful combinations may be retained and reused.

Retained relationships may include:

human collaboration relationships;
human–AI interfaces;
protocols among AI agents;
data formats;
decision paths;
quality standards;
resource-invocation methods;
return-distribution rules;
risk-control processes;
liability-identification methods.

Repeated practice may reduce:

search cost;
communication cost;
verification cost.

It may also increase:

trust;
interface stability;
accumulation of tacit knowledge;
formation of routines;
formation of error-handling paths;
stability of external expectations;
continuity of performance evidence.

These retained relationships form the historical basis of subject formation.

## 9. From External Dependence to Internal Closure

A major mechanism of subject formation is the gradual internalization of external dependencies that are frequent, costly, or critical.

For example, a software-production combination may initially need to find, for every new task:

a requirement interpreter;
an architect;
a programmer;
a tester;
deployment tools;
a liability bearer.

If these temporary dependencies repeatedly generate high costs, the combination may gradually form stable internal relationships.

A possible development path is:

Temporary invocation
→ repeated invocation
→ interface standardization
→ experience accumulation
→ internal-rule formation
→ resource-assurance formation
→ decision-path formation
→ liability-relationship formation
→ internal closure

Therefore:

decision closure emerges by reducing repeated external decision cost;
learning closure emerges by reducing knowledge loss and repeated trial-and-error cost;
resource closure emerges by reducing repeated acquisition cost for critical resources;
evidence closure emerges by reducing repeated capability and credibility proof costs;
exchange closure emerges by reducing repeated explanation and negotiation costs;
liability closure emerges by reducing repeated responsibility-identification and risk-allocation costs.

## 10. Closure Does Not Mean Complete Isolation

A functional subject does not need to be physically self-sufficient or fully independent of its environment.

Real subjects are usually highly dependent on external systems:

firms depend on supply chains;
hospitals depend on pharmaceuticals, energy, and public infrastructure;
AI systems depend on data, computing power, and model services;
states depend on trade and international institutions;
natural persons depend on social division of labor.

The relevant requirement is not total closure, but:

The subject must possess predictable, governable, replaceable, secured, or otherwise controllable relationships to the critical dependencies required for maintaining its core function.

External resources may still be used, but the subject must be able to evaluate:

how they are obtained;
when they are obtained;
their cost;
interruption risk;
substitute availability;
who bears the risk;
who can revoke access;
how core function continues after interruption.

Subject closure is therefore more accurately described as:

Governable closure over critical functional relationships.

## 11. Functional Continuity

Functional continuity is an important condition of subject formation.

It means that a combination can provide an identifiable external function across multiple tasks and time periods.

It does not require:

unchanged members;
unchanged tools;
unchanged AI models;
unchanged resource sources;
identical tasks.

It requires:

an identifiable core function;
continuity of output relationships;
stable external expectations;
internal changes that do not destroy overall function;
continuity of evidence, liability, and learning.

Therefore:

Component continuity ≠ Subject continuity

Legal-name continuity ≠ Functional-subject continuity

## 12. Internal Coupling and External Coupling

A subject boundary is more likely to form when internal relationships are persistently more frequent, stronger, or harder to replace than external relationships.

Two conceptual variables can be introduced:

K_in(A) = Strength of functional, decision, information, and liability relationships inside candidate subject A

K_out(A) = Strength of direct dependency relationships between candidate subject A and external components

A subject boundary is more likely to remain stable when:

K_in(A) >> K_out(A)

Here, >> means “substantially greater than,” not complete closure.

This condition may appear when:

internal relationships occur more frequently;
internal interfaces are more stable;
internal relationships contain stronger path dependence;
internal combinations are harder to replace than external ones;
external actors increasingly interact with the structure as a whole.

## 13. Modularity and Temporary Subjects

The viability of temporary subjects depends heavily on the modularity of tasks and production structures.

Temporary combinations are more likely when:

module boundaries are clear;
interfaces are standardized;
components are highly substitutable;
qualification is easy to verify;
integration cost is low;
liability can be separated;
data can be transferred easily;
temporary combinations can form quickly.

Under such conditions:

C_temporary(A, g, e) < C_stable_maintenance(A, g, e)

Where:

C_temporary(A, g, e): total cost of temporarily assembling structure A for goal g in environment e;
C_stable_maintenance(A, g, e): cost of maintaining a stable subject for that goal and environment.

A temporary subject is not necessarily immature.

It may be the most appropriate structure under specific goals and cost conditions.

Stable subjects are more likely when tasks involve:

high coupling;
important tacit knowledge;
incomplete interfaces;
complex risk;
inseparable liability;
repeated learning;
long-term control of critical resources;
high trust-building cost.

Under such conditions:

C_stable_operation(A, g, e) < C_repeated_recomposition(A, g, e)

Where:

C_stable_operation(A, g, e): cost of operating a stable subject;
C_repeated_recomposition(A, g, e): cost of repeatedly reconstructing a combination for similar goals.

## 14. Temporary and Stable Subjects

Temporary and stable subjects are not necessarily higher and lower stages of the same hierarchy.

They may be different equilibrium outcomes under different goals and cost conditions.

Temporary Subject

A temporary subject often has:

a low-frequency or one-time goal;
a short lifecycle;
easily recombined components;
weak internal closure;
maintenance costs that do not justify long-term preservation;
low-cost exit after completion.
Stable Subject

A stable subject often has:

repeated similar goals;
highly complementary capabilities;
strong internal coordination advantages;
accumulation of tacit knowledge and trust;
complex liability relationships;
requirements for persistent critical resources;
external demand for stable expectations;
maintenance cost lower than repeated recomposition cost.

A subject may evolve from temporary to stable.

A stable subject may also decompose back into temporary combinations.

## 15. Goal-Specific Subjects

A goal-specific subject is optimized for a relatively narrow, stable, and repeated goal space.

Common characteristics include:

highly fixed internal interfaces;
deep specialized knowledge;
low unit-task cost;
short decision paths;
stable output quality;
highly targeted resource allocation;
sensitivity to goal changes;
high transfer cost.

It may be described conceptually as:

A_specific = The candidate subject A that minimizes or substantially reduces C_total(A, g_s, e_s) under narrow goal g_s and environment e_s

A shorter formal expression is:

A_specific = argmin_A C_total(A, g_s, e_s)

Where:

A_specific: goal-specific subject;
g_s: relatively narrow and stable goal;
e_s: relatively stable environment;
argmin_A: the candidate structure A that minimizes or reduces total cost.

A goal-specific subject may be highly stable under fixed conditions but may rapidly lose viability when its goal disappears or technology changes.

## 16. Robust General-Purpose Subjects

A robust general-purpose subject may not achieve the lowest cost on one specific task, but it can maintain acceptable performance across multiple goals and environments.

Common characteristics include:

capability across several goal classes;
ability to identify environmental change;
internal recomposition capacity;
multiple retained strategies;
learning and transfer capacity;
greater redundancy;
more complex internal governance;
higher maintenance cost;
lower single-task efficiency than a specialized subject.

It may be described conceptually as:

A_robust = The candidate subject A that minimizes or substantially reduces average C_total(A, g, e) across goal distribution G and environment distribution E

A shorter formal expression is:

A_robust = argmin_A Average[C_total(A, g, e), g in G, e in E]

Where:

A_robust: robust general-purpose subject;
G: a set or distribution of possible goals;
E: a set or distribution of possible environments;
Average: average performance across multiple goals and environments.

A robust general-purpose subject is optimized for a distribution of goals and environments rather than one fixed task.

## 17. Specialization and Generality Form a Continuum

Goal specialization and cross-goal robustness are not mutually exclusive categories.

They are better understood as a continuum:

Goal specialization <-> Cross-goal robustness

The same subject may show different properties at different levels.

For example:

the overall subject may be a general-purpose engineering subject;
internal modules may be highly specialized;
a specialized module may retain limited transfer capacity;
the subject may increase or decrease specialization as conditions change.

A subject therefore balances:

single-task efficiency;
adaptation range;
redundancy cost;
learning capacity;
transfer cost.

## 18. Response Variety

A subject that must handle a wider range of goals and environments needs greater internal response variety.

A specialized subject only needs to process a narrow state space and can therefore remain relatively simple.

A general-purpose subject requires:

more internal states;
more tools;
more decision paths;
more exception-handling capacity;
stronger learning capacity;
greater resource redundancy.

Therefore:

Robustness is not free.

General-purpose capability usually requires higher:

maintenance cost;
decision cost;
governance cost;
learning cost;
redundancy cost.

Whether a subject should become more general-purpose depends on the relationship between goal-change frequency and general-capability maintenance cost.

## 19. Four Dimensions of Subject Stability

Subject stability should not be represented by one simple axis from weak to strong.

At least four dimensions are needed.

Dimension	One End	Other End
Temporal stability	Temporary subject	Long-term stable subject
Goal range	Goal-specific	Robust general-purpose
Internal control	Highly externally dependent	Highly internally closed
Competitive persistence	Easily replaceable	Stable across multiple environments

A subject may be:

temporary but general-purpose;
stable but highly specialized;
internally closed but vulnerable to new technology;
externally dependent but stable within one platform;
broadly capable but too expensive to maintain;
highly specialized and stable in one fixed environment.

## 20. Internal Stability and External Adaptation

Subject stability includes at least two distinct layers.

Internal Stability

Internal stability concerns whether:

component relationships persist;
decision paths remain operational;
resources remain available;
return distribution remains sustainable;
liability structures remain executable;
conflicts remain manageable;
critical knowledge is preserved;
function continues after component changes.
External Adaptation

External adaptation concerns whether the subject can respond to:

goal change;
price change;
technological change;
resource change;
competition;
institutional change;
external risk.

Subject stability may be represented conceptually as:

S(A) = S_internal(A) * S_external(A) * S_competitive(A)

Where:

S(A): overall stability of subject A;
S_internal(A): continuity of internal function, governance, knowledge, and liability;
S_external(A): capacity to adapt to changes in goals, environments, technology, and resources;
S_competitive(A): capacity to persist against alternative subjects, technologies, and combinations.

Multiplication is used here only to express the idea that severe failure in any one dimension can strongly reduce total stability.

This is a conceptual model, not yet a validated quantitative equation.

## 21. Multi-Subject Competition and Selection

Functional subjects do not form in isolation.

They face:

other functional subjects;
traditional firms;
natural persons;
AI systems;
temporary combinations;
platform structures;
substitute technologies;
new legal forms;
new resource-allocation methods.

Subjects may compete in terms of:

cost;
quality;
speed;
risk;
adaptability;
reputation;
liability reliability;
learning speed;
resource access;
external legitimacy.

Subject formation can be summarized as:

Component-combination variation
→ different combinations perform goals
→ costs and capabilities are exposed
→ repeated effective relationships form routines
→ candidate subject boundaries emerge
→ candidate subjects compete with alternatives
→ some remain stable
→ others divide, are absorbed, or disappear

## 22. Competitive Stability Does Not Mean Global Optimality

A subject surviving competition means only:

It has not yet been replaced under current environmental, institutional, and resource conditions.

It does not mean:

lowest social cost;
highest fairness;
highest legitimacy;
strongest long-term robustness;
globally optimal structure.

Stability may result from:

initial advantage;
standard lock-in;
network effects;
legal protection;
resource monopoly;
exit barriers;
information control;
path dependence.

Therefore:

Competitive survival
≠ social optimum
≠ functional optimum
≠ moral legitimacy

## 23. Competition May Reduce Diversity

Competition among multiple subjects does not always produce better structures.

It may also produce:

winner-take-all outcomes;
standard lock-in;
platform monopoly;
convergence toward one metric;
short-term efficiency at the expense of long-term robustness;
reduced exploration capacity;
elimination of heterogeneous structures;
externalization of cost and liability.

Competition is therefore one selection mechanism, but not a sufficient correction mechanism.

Society may need to preserve:

structural diversity;
exploration space;
low-frequency capabilities;
redundancy;
public-guarantee subjects;
non-market subjects;
alternative competitive paths.

## 24. Path Dependence in Subject Formation

Subject formation is historical.

Once relationships are repeatedly retained, they may generate:

specialized machines;
proprietary data;
tacit knowledge;
specific interfaces;
fixed customer relationships;
internal trust;
brand;
legal structure;
personnel dependence;
switching costs.

These accumulations may increase stability, but they may also create lock-in.

Subject boundaries are therefore shaped not only by current costs, but also by formation history.

The same goals and resources may produce different subjects under different historical paths.

## 25. Power in Subject Formation

Subject formation cannot be explained only by productive efficiency.

Power may shape:

which goals are defined;
which components may enter;
which resources are accessible;
which evidence is recognized;
which subjects receive legal status;
which subjects may exclude competitors;
which members possess real exit rights;
who can modify subject rules;
who can forcibly terminate a subject.

Some subjects may gain non-functional stability by controlling:

resources;
data;
standards;
platforms;
law;
identity;
evaluation systems.

Two sources of stability should therefore be distinguished.

Functional Stability

The subject persists because of lower cost, higher adaptation, and stable output.

Power-Based Stability

The subject persists because it controls resources, rules, information, or exit conditions.

Real subjects often combine both.

## 26. External Identifiability

A stable functional system usually needs to be recognized externally as a whole before it can develop strong subjecthood.

External identifiability may include:

one interface;
one price or condition structure;
one delivery channel;
one liability entry point;
one reputation record;
one resource-request structure;
one service-condition structure;
direct negotiation with external subjects;
external expectation of continuity across tasks.

External recognition does not automatically create a subject.

However, if a system can never be recognized as a whole, its exchange, liability, and reputation closure will usually remain weak.

## 27. Cost, Preference, and Reservation Conditions

A stable functional subject must gradually form its own:

cost structure;
risk structure;
time preferences;
resource preferences;
task-selection structure;
reservation conditions;
pricing or resource-request methods.

It must be able to judge:

which tasks to accept;
which tasks to reject;
minimum acceptable return;
maximum acceptable risk;
acceptable time commitment;
required resources;
acceptable liability;
exit conditions;
whether short-term return should be sacrificed for long-term capability.

When a system can form and enforce its own reservation conditions, it moves further from passive component status toward active subject status.

## 28. Evidence Continuity

Subject formation requires evidence continuity across tasks.

Evidence may include:

performance records;
failure records;
resource-use records;
decision records;
quality records;
risk records;
version histories;
component-change records;
return-and-loss records;
liability-resolution records.

Evidence continuity enables external actors to determine:

whether this is the same subject;
whether capability is stable;
whether performance is improving;
whether performance is deteriorating;
whether further resources should be allocated;
whether liability should be imposed;
whether subject continuity still exists.

Without evidence continuity, a functional system will struggle to develop long-term credibility and stable external relationships.

## 29. Return, Loss, and Liability Closure

A functional subject must form structures for receiving returns, bearing losses, and carrying liability.

A return structure must answer:

who receives external returns;
how internal returns are distributed;
how delayed returns are preserved;
who owns knowledge and data benefits;
whether exiting members retain future rights;
how shared subject-level resources accumulate.

A loss structure must answer:

who bears task failure;
who bears cost overruns;
who bears delays;
who bears external harm;
how internal errors are allocated;
whether insurance or compensation exists.

A liability structure must answer:

who defines goals;
who approves tasks;
who controls resources;
who executes;
who verifies;
who can stop the system;
who receives returns;
who bears losses;
who performs remediation;
who bears final external liability.

A system that can receive returns but cannot bear losses or liability has incomplete subjecthood.

## 30. Formation of Internal Governance

A stable subject needs internal governance to handle:

goal selection;
task acceptance;
resource allocation;
component entry;
component exit;
return distribution;
risk bearing;
decision conflict;
evidence auditing;
liability handling;
rule modification;
subject merger;
subject division;
subject exit.

Governance may involve:

human management;
voting;
contracts;
hierarchy;
AI coordination;
polycentric negotiation;
algorithmic execution;
hybrid structures.

No single governance mechanism fits all subjects.

Governance structure itself affects formation cost and long-term stability.

## 31. AI’s Dual Effect on Subject Formation

AI may both stabilize subjects and increase recomposition.

AI May Promote Stable Subject Formation

AI may reduce:

internal coordination cost;
evidence-maintenance cost;
decision cost;
learning cost;
risk-identification cost;
interface-maintenance cost;
cross-organizational coordination cost.

AI may also help:

preserve long-term knowledge;
maintain subject continuity;
form dynamic prices;
manage complex internal relationships;
trace liability;
support adaptation across goals.
AI May Promote Subject Recomposition and Decomposition

AI may also reduce:

external search cost;
capability-identification cost;
temporary-matching cost;
contracting cost;
component-replacement cost;
module-integration cost.

This may lead to:

decomposition of fixed jobs;
movement of functions outside firms;
growth of temporary subjects;
more fluid subject boundaries;
loss of economic justification for some traditional organizations.

The result in the AI era depends on which cost falls more:

AI-caused reduction in internal coordination cost;
AI-caused reduction in external recomposition cost.

If internal coordination cost falls more, larger or more stable subjects may expand.

If external recomposition cost falls more, subjects may become more modular, temporary, and fluid.

## 32. Natural Persons and Subject Formation

Natural persons may occupy different positions within functional-subject formation:

independently forming a complete functional subject;
forming a joint subject with AI;
providing different capabilities to multiple subjects;
participating in governance;
carrying final liability;
participating temporarily without becoming a stable component;
leaving a subject while retaining capability records;
leaving while retaining some long-term return rights.

Subject stability must not depend on eliminating a natural person’s basic exit rights.

Otherwise, a functional subject may become:

a labor-dependence structure;
a data-control structure;
a capability-lock-in structure;
an identity-control structure;
a new form of algorithmic bureaucracy.

Subject formation should therefore preserve:

real exit rights;
portability of capability records;
fundamental rights independent of subject membership;
appeal rights;
refusal rights;
clear boundaries of return and liability.

## 33. Subject Evolution of Existing Organizations

Traditional organizations may evolve in several ways.

Whole-Organization Stability

The existing organization remains a stable functional subject.

Internal Differentiation

Several relatively independent functional subjects emerge inside one organization.

External Absorption

The organization absorbs external AI systems, machines, data, or people and forms a new subject boundary.

Cross-Organizational Integration

Components from several organizations form a new cross-organizational functional subject.

Functional Externalization

An internal function becomes an independent external subject.

Subject Degradation

An organization keeps its legal name but loses functional, decision, evidence, or liability closure.

Platform Absorption

A formerly independent subject becomes controlled by a platform in pricing, tasks, evaluation, and exit, and gradually degrades into a platform component.

Therefore:

Continuity of legal organization ≠ Continuity of functional subject

## 34. Reversibility of Subject Formation

Subject formation is not irreversible.

A stable functional subject may divide, degrade, or disappear because:

demand for its function disappears;
capability complementarity declines;
internal coordination cost rises;
critical resources are lost;
components become easier to substitute;
AI sharply reduces recomposition cost;
liability structures fail;
evidence continuity breaks;
internal governance collapses;
core components exit;
external actors stop recognizing the structure as a whole;
a new subject adapts better;
law or governance forces division;
the power structure supporting the subject disappears.

Subject formation and subject dissolution should be studied within one dynamic framework.

## 35. Preliminary Expression of Subject-Formation Tendency

A candidate subject’s formation tendency may be represented conceptually as:

F(A) = f(R_f, C_c, K_i, D_c, L_c, E_c, R_s, A_e, C_r, C_g, C_x, D_o)

Where:

F(A): subject-formation tendency of candidate structure A;
R_f: degree of functional repetition;
C_c: degree of capability complementarity;
K_i: stability of internal relationships;
D_c: degree of decision closure;
L_c: learning and evidence continuity;
E_c: exchange and external identifiability;
R_s: stability of resource and liability structures;
A_e: adaptation to goals and environments;
C_r: cost advantage of temporary recomposition over stable maintenance;
C_g: internal governance cost;
C_x: component-conflict cost;
D_o: degree of direct external control.

Positive-direction variables include:

R_f, C_c, K_i, D_c, L_c, E_c, R_s, A_e

Negative-direction variables include:

C_r, C_g, C_x, D_o

This is not a completed mathematical model.

It does not imply that variables of different units can be directly added, nor that all variables have equal weight.

It is only a starting set of variables for later formalization.

## 36. Preliminary Expression of Subject Stability

Subject stability may be represented conceptually as:

S(A) = S_internal(A) * S_external(A) * S_competitive(A)

Where:

S(A): overall stability of subject A;
S_internal(A): continuity of internal function, governance, knowledge, and liability structures;
S_external(A): capacity to adapt to changes in goals, environments, technology, and resources;
S_competitive(A): capacity to persist against substitute subjects, technologies, and combinations.

Multiplication is used only to express the idea that failure in any one dimension can strongly reduce overall stability.

This is a conceptual model, not a validated quantitative equation, and it does not imply equal weights.

Subject stability does not directly represent social value.

Social value must be evaluated separately:

V_social(A) = The comprehensive value produced by subject A for society

V_social(A) may include:

total social cost;
externalities;
effects on natural-person rights;
fairness;
innovation capacity;
long-term robustness;
concentration of power;
replaceability;
social legitimacy;
effects on social diversity;
effects on future capability development.

Therefore:

S(A) != V_social(A)

This means:

a subject may be highly stable but produce low social value;
a subject may survive competition because of monopoly or power protection;
a subject may be highly efficient while seriously harming natural-person rights;
a subject may be unstable in the short term but valuable for innovation and social exploration.

Later research must therefore separately evaluate:

subject stability;
functional efficiency;
competitive persistence;
social value;
social legitimacy.

## 37. Core Stages of Subject Formation

Subject formation can be summarized in the following stages.

Stage One: Goal Emergence

A goal appears in social practice.

Stage Two: Combination Attempts

Different capabilities, resources, natural persons, AI systems, and organizational components form multiple candidate combinations.

Stage Three: Cost Exposure

Different combinations expose quality, speed, risk, liability, and total realization cost through practice.

Stage Four: Relationship Retention

Relationships that perform well, or that receive institutional support, are repeatedly reused.

Stage Five: Relationship Accumulation

Interfaces, knowledge, routines, trust, resources, and liability relationships gradually stabilize.

Stage Six: Internalization of External Dependencies

Frequent, costly, or critical external dependencies are gradually internalized.

Stage Seven: Closure Formation

Decision, learning, resource, evidence, exchange, and liability closure gradually form.

Stage Eight: Formation of Adaptation Direction

The subject develops toward goal specialization, robust generality, or a mixed structure.

Stage Nine: Competitive and Institutional Selection

The subject competes with other structures and is influenced by law, power, and path dependence.

Stage Ten: Dynamic Stability

The subject forms a temporary or long-term stable boundary.

Stage Eleven: Continued Evolution

The subject continues to expand, contract, divide, absorb, recompose, or disappear.

## 38. Subject Formation and Social Legitimacy

Subject-formation theory explains how subjects emerge. It does not automatically prove that a subject should exist.

Even if a subject is:

low-cost;
efficient;
functionally stable;
competitively successful;

it may still:

violate natural-person rights;
create monopoly;
control information;
restrict exit;
externalize risk;
hide liability;
eliminate social diversity;
reinforce power concentration.

The following must therefore be evaluated separately:

formation cause;
functional efficiency;
competitive stability;
power structure;
social legitimacy.

Stable subject ≠ Legitimate subject

Efficient subject ≠ Subject that should be permanently preserved

## 40. Current Conclusion

Functional-subject formation can be summarized as:

Social goals and environmental changes
→ multiple component combinations attempt to realize goals
→ different combinations expose total social functional-realization costs
→ effective relationships are retained through repeated practice
→ interfaces, knowledge, routines, resources, and liability relationships accumulate
→ frequent external dependencies are gradually internalized
→ decision, learning, resource, evidence, exchange, and liability closure form
→ the subject develops toward goal specialization or robust generality
→ it is selected under multi-subject competition, institutional constraints, power relationships, and path dependence
→ it forms temporary or long-term stable boundaries
→ it continues to evolve, divide, absorb, or disappear as goals, technology, and costs change

Therefore:

A functional subject is not a predefined fixed entity. It is a stable functional structure formed through the interaction of social production, division of labor, technology, exchange, liability, competition, institutions, and power.

The central issue in subject formation is not merely why organizations exist.

It is:

How society compresses the total cost of realizing goals through long-term practice, and how repeatedly effective capability relationships become stable functional closure capable of independently adapting to external goals.
