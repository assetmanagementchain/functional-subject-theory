## 1. The Problem Addressed by the Theory

Modern society usually assumes several basic types of subjects:

natural persons;
firms;
governments;
legal organizations;
communities;
platforms;
other organizations predefined by law or administration.

These categories have had strong explanatory power in industrial society. However, they may become increasingly coarse under the development of AI, automation, platform coordination, and human–AI production.

Stable production functions are increasingly likely to be provided by combinations such as:

part of a natural person’s capabilities;
collaboration among multiple natural persons;
one or more AI systems;
automated tools;
equipment;
data;
rules;
knowledge bases;
platform interfaces;
liability mechanisms;
cross-organizational coordination relationships.

These combinations may provide persistent functions even when their boundaries do not coincide with the boundary of a natural person, firm, or government department.

Functional Subject Theory therefore raises a more fundamental question:

In future production, exchange, governance, and responsibility systems, should effective subjects still be assumed to be natural persons, firms, and governments, or should they be identified according to stable functional systems that actually emerge from production?

## 2. Core Hypothesis

The core hypothesis of Functional Subject Theory is:

When a functional system can continuously provide a stable external function and form relatively complete structures of decision-making, resources, exchange, evidence, and liability, it may become an effective subject.

Such a subject may consist of:

one natural person;
part of a natural person’s capabilities combined with tools;
stable collaboration among multiple natural persons;
one AI system;
multiple collaborating AI systems;
a stable human–AI combination;
a production system composed of automated equipment, data, and maintenance personnel;
a stable functional network crossing multiple traditional organizations.

Therefore, subject boundaries are not determined only by biological boundaries, legal registration, or administrative affiliation.

They may be jointly determined by:

stable functional boundaries;
decision boundaries;
resource-control boundaries;
cost-and-return boundaries;
exchange boundaries;
evidence-continuity boundaries;
liability boundaries;
internal governance boundaries.

## 3. Functional Systems and Functional Subjects

Not every functional system can become a subject.

A temporary combination of tools, a one-time project collaboration, or a single functional module may possess production capability without constituting an independent subject.

A functional system only means:

Certain components jointly produce a function.

A functional subject must additionally possess:

persistence;
external identifiability;
a relatively independent decision loop;
the ability to control or coordinate resources;
its own cost structure;
its own preferences or choice structure;
pricing or contracting capacity;
continuity of performance evidence;
structures for receiving returns and bearing losses;
a liability structure;
the capacity to evolve, divide, merge, or exit.

Therefore:

Functional system ≠ Functional subject

Functional module ≠ Functional subject

Temporary collaboration ≠ Stable functional subject

A stable functional subject must form a sufficient degree of closure in function, decision-making, exchange, and liability.

## 4. The Position of Natural Persons

Functional Subject Theory does not eliminate natural persons and does not reduce them to dispatchable tools.

Natural persons remain the basic subjects of:

fundamental rights;
bodily autonomy;
lived experience;
consent;
refusal;
appeal;
privacy;
personal welfare;
autonomous choice over whether to participate in a production relationship;
control over their own time and capabilities.

Functional Subject Theory distinguishes between two layers.

Natural Persons as Subjects of Rights and Experience

A natural person’s basic rights should not depend on production capacity, task performance, or market price.

Functional Subjects as Operational Subjects of Production and Governance

Functional subjects may undertake:

tasks;
production;
exchange;
coordination;
pricing;
resource organization;
evidence accumulation;
operational responsibility;
organizational evolution.

A natural person may participate in multiple functional subjects at the same time.

A functional subject may also consist of multiple natural persons, AI systems, machines, and organizational components.

Therefore:

A natural person does not have to be identical to one indivisible production subject.

A functional subject does not have to be identical to one complete natural person or traditional legal entity.

## 5. Capability, Willingness, and Supply

Functional Subject Theory distinguishes among:

capability;
willingness to provide;
reservation price;
actual supply;
transaction formation;
delivery outcome.

Possessing a capability does not imply willingness to provide it.

Whether a natural person or functional subject is willing to accept a task may depend on:

direct cost;
opportunity cost;
time cost;
risk;
expected return;
learning value;
strategic value;
degree of autonomy;
reputation;
long-term return;
effects on future capability accumulation.

Therefore:

Capability ≠ Supply

Willingness to supply ≠ Transaction formation

A transaction forms only when the acceptable contract ranges of the relevant subjects overlap.

A functional subject must possess its own cost structure and price curve rather than being externally assigned solely according to capability.

## 6. Price as a Multidimensional Contract

Exchange among functional subjects does not have to occur through money alone.

Price may be a multidimensional contract containing:

monetary return;
resource access;
computing power;
equipment access;
data access;
time flexibility;
risk allocation;
learning opportunities;
reputation;
decision rights;
future revenue rights;
long-term participation rights;
intellectual property;
exit conditions.

Exchange among functional subjects is therefore more accurately understood as:

A dynamic equilibrium among multidimensional costs, returns, risks, and rights.

Markets may be one discovery mechanism, but they are not the only possible mechanism.

## 7. Subject Formation as an Endogenous Process

Functional subjects are not necessarily predefined by theory or created by administrative authority.

They may gradually emerge through sustained production.

One possible formation process is:

Capability fragments
→ repeated collaboration
→ capability complementarity
→ lower internal coordination cost
→ stable external output
→ formation of a decision loop
→ formation of evidence continuity
→ formation of return and liability boundaries
→ formation of a candidate functional subject

Whether a subject boundary remains stable may depend on:

internal coordination costs;
external transaction costs;
recomposition costs;
frequency of repeated functions;
degree of capability complementarity;
resource dependencies;
liability structures;
the effect of AI on search, matching, verification, and coordination costs.

When repeated recomposition is expensive, stable subjects are more likely to form.

When AI significantly reduces recomposition costs, traditional firm boundaries may become more fluid.

Subject formation is therefore a dynamic equilibrium process.

## 8. Relationship to Firms

A firm may be understood as one historical implementation of a functional subject, but not the only one.

Traditional firms usually form stable boundaries through:

internal command;
long-term employment;
asset control;
branding;
legal liability;
managerial hierarchy;
internal knowledge accumulation;
external contracting capacity.

Future functional subjects may not depend entirely on these structures.

For example, a stable software-production subject may consist of:

a product decision-maker;
a coding AI;
a testing AI;
deployment tools;
a customer-feedback system;
cloud resources;
liability insurance;
limited human review.

It may cross multiple firms or be smaller than a firm.

Functional Subject Theory therefore does not merely study internal firm structure. It studies:

Which stable functional systems become independent subjects and which remain modules.

## 9. Relationship to Markets, Planning, and Polycentric Governance

Functional Subject Theory is not itself a single allocation mechanism.

It studies the underlying entities upon which allocation mechanisms operate.

Functional subjects may coordinate through:

markets;
auctions;
contracts;
platform matching;
public budgeting;
administrative coordination;
community governance;
polycentric governance;
planning;
negotiation;
hybrid mechanisms.

The conceptual hierarchy is:

Subject ontology
→ value and price formation
→ allocation and coordination mechanisms
→ governance, auditing, and correction

Polycentric governance studies how multiple governance centers coordinate.

Functional Subject Theory asks a more basic question:

What will future governance centers themselves consist of?

They may no longer be limited to natural persons, government departments, or traditional organizations. They may also be stable human–AI functional systems.

## 10. Relationship to the Capability Approach

The capability approach emphasizes what people are genuinely able to do and choose, and whether they possess real freedom.

Functional Subject Theory accepts this value foundation but further distinguishes among:

a person possessing a capability;
whether the person is willing to provide it;
whether the capability enters a functional subject;
whether the functional subject is willing to accept a task;
whether a contract forms.

The capability approach mainly asks:

What real capabilities and freedoms does a person possess?

Functional Subject Theory additionally asks:

How do capabilities enter production structures and form subjects with independent cost, preference, exchange, and liability structures?

The two approaches are not contradictory.

The capability approach is closer to the layer of natural-person rights and welfare.

Functional Subject Theory is closer to the layer of production-subject formation.

## 11. Why AI Changes Subject Structure

AI may significantly reduce:

capability-identification costs;
search costs;
matching costs;
contracting costs;
coordination costs;
supervision costs;
evidence-recording costs;
knowledge-reuse costs;
module-recomposition costs;
cross-organizational collaboration costs.

As these costs decline, the boundaries of traditional firms and fixed organizations may no longer be the most efficient boundaries.

New functional subjects may be:

smaller;
more flexible;
cross-organizational;
composed of multiple AI systems;
composed of partial capabilities of natural persons;
continuously reorganized according to production needs;
restabilized through repeated collaboration.

AI therefore does not merely replace certain jobs.

It may change:

What can become an economic and governance subject.

## 12. Phenomena the Theory Seeks to Explain

Functional Subject Theory seeks to explain:

why some human–AI combinations become stable over time;
why some project combinations develop into new organizations;
why some traditional firms become decomposed;
why some platform-control structures behave more like subjects than individual firms;
why some AI systems gradually acquire independent pricing and delivery capacity;
why cross-organizational collaboration may form new stable functional boundaries;
why a natural person’s capabilities may enter multiple production subjects at once;
why legal subjects and actual production subjects may increasingly diverge;
why new liability structures must pass through AI, natural-person, and organizational components;
why future organizations may form around stable functions rather than fixed identities.

## 13. Core Value of the Theory

The main value of Functional Subject Theory is not to prescribe an ideal society in advance.

It provides a new unit of analysis:

Instead of assuming that natural persons, firms, and governments are the only subjects, it observes which stable functional systems actually form decision, resource, exchange, and liability closure in production.

The theory may be applied to:

future organization studies;
AI-agent architecture;
human–AI collaboration systems;
platform-economy analysis;
firm-boundary research;
public governance;
liability-system design;
AI legal-subject debates;
polycentric governance;
new market-mechanism design.

## 14. Current Open Questions

The theory is still in draft form and currently faces at least the following open questions:

How stable must a function be before a system can be recognized as a subject?
How can the boundary between a module and a subject be measured?
How should independent decision capacity be defined?
How should internal components distribute returns?
How should internal components bear losses?
Can AI components independently bear liability?
Must a subject possess independent pricing capacity?
Must a subject be able to refuse tasks?
How does a subject form long-term preferences?
How can subjects be prevented from developing self-preservation and monopoly structures?
When a natural person exits a functional subject, how can capability records and revenue rights remain portable?
When should a functional subject be forcibly divided or terminated?
Must functional subjects and legal subjects correspond one-to-one?
How should externalities among functional subjects be handled?
How can real-world cases and simulations validate subject-formation laws?

## 15. Current Theoretical Position

Functional Subject Theory can currently be described as:

A theory of endogenous functional subjects for the AI era.

It studies:

how subjects form;
why subjects remain stable;
how subject boundaries change;
how subjects form prices and preferences;
how subjects participate in exchange;
how subjects bear responsibility;
how subjects evolve and exit;
how natural-person rights can be separated from production-subject structures and protected.

It is not yet a completed social-system design.

It is a theoretical framework for studying future structures of production, exchange, governance, and responsibility.
