## 1. Purpose of This Document

This document studies:

How the boundaries of functional subjects form, how they can be identified, how they change, and how they fail.

Traditional societies usually identify subjects through boundaries such as:

biological body boundaries;
legal identity boundaries;
corporate-registration boundaries;
administrative-organization boundaries;
ownership boundaries;
employment boundaries;
geographic boundaries.

These boundaries remain important, but in AI systems, human–AI collaboration, platform production, and cross-organizational networks, they may fail to represent the actual functional structure.

A functional subject that performs real production, decision-making, and liability bearing may:

be smaller than one firm;
span several firms;
include several natural persons;
use only part of a natural person’s capabilities;
include several AI systems and automated systems;
rely on external resources;
depend on another legal subject for final liability;
change internal components across tasks.

A functional-subject boundary therefore cannot be represented by one static line.

It should be understood as a dynamic structure composed of multiple relational boundaries.

## 2. Core Proposition

A functional-subject boundary is:

A relatively closed region of decision, resource, information, exchange, evidence, return, risk, liability, and governance relationships formed around a stable function.

A subject boundary is not a completely sealed wall.

It is better understood as an identifiable relational interface that distinguishes:

which relationships are primarily completed inside the subject;
which relationships require external exchange;
which resources are controlled by the subject;
which decisions are made by the subject;
which outcomes are attributed to the subject;
which liabilities are borne by the subject or its supporting structures;
which changes count as internal evolution of the same subject;
which changes mean that the subject has divided, been absorbed, or disappeared.

## 3. A Subject Boundary Is Not a Single Boundary

A functional subject may have several distinct boundaries:

functional boundary;
decision boundary;
resource boundary;
information boundary;
capability boundary;
exchange boundary;
price-and-preference boundary;
evidence boundary;
return boundary;
loss-and-risk boundary;
liability boundary;
governance boundary;
temporal-continuity boundary;
external-identification boundary.

These boundaries may:

fully coincide;
partially coincide;
be nested within each other;
cross traditional organizations;
change across tasks and environments;
form several subjects at different levels.

Therefore:

Legal boundary ≠ Functional boundary

Ownership boundary ≠ Decision boundary

Resource-use boundary ≠ Liability boundary

Organizational-name boundary ≠ Subject-continuity boundary

## 4. Functional Boundary

The functional boundary answers:

Which components jointly maintain an identifiable and stable function?

The functional boundary may include:

functional inputs;
core processing;
outputs;
quality standards;
goal-adaptation range;
failure handling;
functional maintenance;
functional updating.

If removing a component prevents the subject from maintaining its core function, that component is more likely to lie inside the functional boundary.

If a component can be replaced at low cost without disrupting subject continuity, it is more likely to lie near the periphery.

A functional boundary is therefore not merely a list of members.

It is a structure of functional dependence.

## 5. Core Functions and Peripheral Functions

Functions inside a subject can be divided into core and peripheral functions.

Core Functions

Core functions are necessary for maintaining subject identity and external value.

If a core function is lost, the subject may:

lose its primary output;
no longer be externally recognized;
become unable to perform commitments;
transform into another subject;
effectively disappear.
Peripheral Functions

Peripheral functions support subject operation but may be:

outsourced;
replaced;
temporarily invoked;
shared with other subjects;
removed without changing subject identity.

Subject boundaries are usually more stable around core functions and more fluid around peripheral functions.

## 6. Decision Boundary

The decision boundary answers:

Which decisions are formed inside the subject, and which are directly controlled by external subjects?

Relevant decisions include:

whether to accept a task;
how to interpret a goal;
how to select a method;
how to allocate resources;
how to handle exceptions;
how to form a price;
how much risk to accept;
how to modify internal components;
how to distribute returns;
whether to continue existing;
whether to exit a relationship.

If a system cannot independently make any decision about its critical tasks, it is usually closer to a tool or module than a subject.

However, a subject does not need to possess all decision rights.

A more accurate test is:

Does the subject possess stable and executable control over the critical decisions required to maintain its core function?

## 7. Decision Rights and Execution Rights

Decision rights and execution rights must be distinguished.

A component may:

perform many operations;
process large amounts of information;
strongly influence outcomes;

while not deciding:

the goal;
the resource allocation;
the accepted risk;
the exit condition;
the liability arrangement.

For example, an AI system may perform substantial execution while its goals, permissions, and stopping conditions are fully controlled by an external organization.

Therefore:

Strong execution capacity ≠ Strong subject independence

Boundary analysis must identify:

who defines the goal;
who approves the goal;
who chooses the method;
who can override a decision;
who can stop the system;
who can modify the rules.

## 8. Resource Boundary

The resource boundary answers:

Over which critical resources does the subject possess stable control, coordination, or assurance?

Resource relationships may involve:

ownership;
usage rights;
access rights;
scheduling rights;
return rights;
exclusion rights;
sharing rights;
revocation rights;
temporary authorization;
substitute assurance.

A subject does not need to own all of its resources.

It may depend on external resources as long as critical resource relationships are:

predictable;
coordinable;
replaceable;
secured;
governable.

A resource boundary is therefore not an asset list.

It is a structure of resource-control relationships.

## 9. Resource Dependence and Subject Independence

High dependence on external resources does not automatically eliminate subjecthood.

Further questions are required:

Can the external resource be withdrawn unilaterally at any time?
Does the subject have substitutes?
Can the subject negotiate terms?
Would interruption immediately destroy the core function?
Can the resource provider directly control subject decisions?
Does the subject bear the consequences of resource use?
Is the resource relationship stable over time?

If an external provider can fully determine the subject’s tasks, prices, evaluation, and exit, the candidate subject may be only a component of the external subject.

## 10. Information Boundary

The information boundary answers:

Which information belongs to the internal state of the subject, and which information is exchanged through external interfaces?

The information boundary may include:

internal knowledge;
decision grounds;
data;
model states;
cost information;
risk information;
user information;
performance records;
internal rules;
liability records.

An information boundary does not imply that all information should remain secret.

It is mainly used to identify:

which information maintains subject continuity;
which information may be externally shared;
which information must remain auditable;
which information belongs to natural persons;
which information may be carried after subject exit;
who can modify or delete information.

## 11. Information Control and False Subject Independence

A system may appear formally independent while another platform fully controls its critical information.

A platform may control:

customer relationships;
historical ratings;
capability evidence;
pricing information;
transaction records;
identity verification;
ranking;
task access.

In such a case, the candidate subject may have substantial execution capacity but still fail to form a genuinely independent boundary.

Therefore:

Information dependence may create a deeper form of control than asset dependence.

Subject-boundary analysis must identify information control, not merely legal ownership.

## 12. Capability Boundary

The capability boundary answers:

Which capabilities are durably available to the subject, and which are only temporary external services?

Internal capabilities may come from:

natural persons;
AI systems;
machines;
data;
rules;
knowledge bases;
long-term external collaboration structures.

Whether a capability lies inside the subject can be assessed by asking:

Is it stably available over time?
Does it participate in the core function?
Does it accumulate evidence jointly with the subject?
Does it affect subject cost and pricing?
Does it participate in returns and liability?
Can it freely exit?
Can the subject continue after it exits?

The capability boundary may differ completely from the boundary of a natural person.

A natural person may contribute only part of their capabilities to one subject while retaining other capabilities for other subjects.

## 13. Exchange Boundary

The exchange boundary answers:

Through what collective identity does the subject transact, negotiate, or request resources from external parties?

An exchange boundary may appear as:

unified pricing;
unified contracts;
unified service conditions;
unified resource requests;
unified delivery;
unified dispute resolution;
unified exit conditions.

If external parties must contract separately with every internal component, the collective exchange closure may be weak.

If the collection can form and execute commitments as a whole, its exchange boundary is clearer.

## 14. Price and Preference Boundary

The price-and-preference boundary answers:

Can the collection form its own task choices, cost judgments, and reservation conditions?

Relevant questions include:

Who calculates cost?
Who defines minimum acceptable return?
Who evaluates risk?
Who accepts tasks?
Who rejects tasks?
Who determines long-term strategy?
How are internal preferences aggregated?
Can an external party force changes in pricing?

If pricing and task selection are entirely controlled by an external platform, the candidate subject may not possess an independent preference or exchange boundary.

## 15. Evidence Boundary

The evidence boundary answers:

Which records jointly demonstrate that this is the same subject and support continuity of capability, reputation, and liability?

Evidence may include:

task records;
quality records;
failure records;
decision logs;
resource-use records;
version records;
internal component changes;
return-and-loss records;
liability-resolution records.

The evidence boundary must answer:

To whom do the records belong?
Who can access them?
Who can modify them?
Who can carry them elsewhere?
How are they divided after subject separation?
How are they preserved after subject exit?
How can natural persons carry their own capability records?

Without an evidence boundary, external recognition of subject continuity becomes difficult.

## 16. Return Boundary

The return boundary answers:

Who receives, accumulates, and distributes the returns produced by the subject?

Relevant distinctions include:

natural-person returns;
component returns;
shared subject-level returns;
delayed returns;
knowledge returns;
data returns;
brand returns;
future return rights;
public returns;
externality-related returns.

If all returns flow directly to an external controller and the internal collection cannot accumulate any subject-level resources, its economic boundary may be weak.

Subject-level returns may be used to:

maintain shared capability;
form risk reserves;
update machines;
develop knowledge;
pay liability costs;
support long-term goals.

## 17. Loss and Risk Boundary

The loss-and-risk boundary answers:

After failure, which losses are borne by the subject, and which are shifted to internal components or external society?

Relevant losses include:

financial loss;
time loss;
resource loss;
reputation loss;
legal liability;
environmental damage;
harm to natural persons;
data leakage;
long-term risk;
systemic risk.

If a collection receives returns but externalizes all losses, both its subject closure and its social legitimacy are problematic.

## 18. Liability Boundary

The liability boundary answers:

After an outcome occurs, who must explain, repair, compensate, or accept restriction?

Liability may include:

goal-definition liability;
design liability;
data liability;
decision liability;
execution liability;
supervision liability;
approval liability;
maintenance liability;
stopping liability;
compensation liability;
long-term-impact liability.

In a complex functional subject, liability may span:

natural persons;
AI systems;
software developers;
data providers;
machine providers;
platforms;
legal entities;
insurance structures;
public institutions.

The liability boundary is therefore often not one point.

It is a liability-penetration network.

## 19. Liability Penetration

Liability penetration means:

When a functional subject consists of multiple components, liability can be traced through the actual relationships of control, benefit, decision, and risk.

Liability cannot simply stop at claims such as:

“the system decided”;
“the algorithm decided”;
“the group decided”;
“the platform is only an intermediary”;
“the component is only a tool.”

Analysis must trace:

who could change the goal;
who controlled the resources;
who could veto a decision;
who received the main benefit;
who knew the risks;
who had the capacity to stop the process;
who deliberately ignored anomalies;
who carried final assurance.

## 20. Governance Boundary

The governance boundary answers:

Who can create, execute, modify, and audit the subject’s internal rules?

Governance includes:

member entry;
member exit;
resource allocation;
permission allocation;
return distribution;
risk bearing;
rule modification;
evidence auditing;
decision override;
subject merger;
subject division;
subject termination.

If a collection cannot modify any of its own internal rules, its governance independence is usually weak.

However, governance may be distributed across several centers and does not require one sole controller.

## 21. Temporal-Continuity Boundary

The temporal-continuity boundary answers:

After internal components change, under what conditions is the structure still considered the same subject?

Subject continuity does not require:

identical personnel;
identical AI models;
identical machines;
identical legal names;
completely unchanged function.

Continuity may instead be assessed through:

continuity of core function;
continuity of decision structure;
continuity of evidence records;
continuity of liability bearing;
continuity of external recognition;
continuity of internal knowledge;
continuity of contracts;
continuity of return-and-loss relationships.

## 22. Determining Whether It Is the Same Subject

Subject continuity may be represented conceptually as:

Identity_continuity(A, t1, t2) = f(F_c, D_c, E_c, L_c, G_c, X_c)

Where:

F_c: continuity of core function;
D_c: continuity of decision structure;
E_c: continuity of evidence;
L_c: continuity of liability;
G_c: continuity of governance;
X_c: continuity of external recognition.

This is only a conceptual model.

It does not imply that these variables can be directly added, or that subject continuity always has one uniquely correct answer.

Different legal, economic, and social contexts may use different continuity standards.

## 23. External-Identification Boundary

The external-identification boundary answers:

Does the external world interact with the collection as one whole object?

External identification may be formed through:

a name;
an interface;
a brand;
an address;
an account;
a unified contract;
a unified reputation;
unified delivery;
one liability entry point;
an expectation of continued existence.

External identification is not a sufficient condition for subject formation.

However, if the external world can never treat a collection as a whole, its pricing, liability, evidence, and continuity are difficult to stabilize.

## 24. Boundary Alignment and Boundary Misalignment

The boundaries of a mature subject do not necessarily fully coincide.

For example:

the functional boundary may span several firms;
legal liability may be concentrated in one firm;
data may be controlled by a platform;
natural persons may perform actual execution;
returns may primarily flow to capital owners;
risk may be borne by society.

This creates boundary misalignment.

Boundary misalignment may lead to:

liability avoidance;
separation of returns and risks;
hidden actual controllers;
misclassification of labor relationships;
underestimation of platform responsibility;
ambiguous AI liability;
incorrect identification of the regulated subject.

## 25. Degree of Boundary Misalignment

Boundary misalignment may be represented conceptually as:

Boundary_misalignment(A) = f(F_b, D_b, R_b, I_b, P_b, E_b, L_b, G_b)

Where:

F_b: functional boundary;
D_b: decision boundary;
R_b: resource boundary;
I_b: information boundary;
P_b: return-and-price boundary;
E_b: evidence boundary;
L_b: liability boundary;
G_b: governance boundary.

The greater the divergence among these boundaries, the more important it becomes to investigate:

who actually controls;
who actually benefits;
who bears the risk;
who should be regulated;
who can exit;
who possesses real subjecthood.

## 26. Nested Subjects

A functional subject may contain other functional subjects.

Examples include:

a hospital subject containing diagnosis, surgery, and medication-management subjects;
a software-production subject containing requirement, coding, and testing subjects;
a government system containing several public-function subjects;
a large AI platform containing several independent service subjects.

Nested subjects require analysis of:

local autonomy;
resource dependence;
decision-override relationships;
return distribution;
liability penetration;
possibility of separation.

## 27. Overlapping Subjects

The same component may participate in several subjects simultaneously.

A natural person may participate in:

a research subject;
a teaching subject;
a business subject;
a public-governance subject.

The same AI system may also serve several subjects.

Overlapping subjects may create:

time conflicts;
resource conflicts;
data conflicts;
interest conflicts;
liability conflicts;
evidence-ownership conflicts;
intellectual-property conflicts.

The boundary contracts between a component and multiple subjects must therefore be explicit.

## 28. Temporary and Stable Boundaries

A temporary subject boundary often has:

a short lifecycle;
limited resource control;
weak evidence continuity;
a narrow liability range;
a clearly defined goal;
automatic dissolution after task completion.

A stable subject boundary often:

spans several tasks;
preserves long-term knowledge;
forms a stable external interface;
develops persistent cost and return structures;
bears long-term liability;
can evolve internal components.

A temporary boundary is not inferior.

It may be more efficient for low-frequency goals.

## 29. Boundaries of Specialized and General-Purpose Subjects

Goal-specific subjects often have clearer boundaries because:

the goal range is narrow;
interfaces are stable;
core components are identifiable;
resource relationships are fixed;
liability scope is limited.

Robust general-purpose subjects may have more ambiguous boundaries because:

their goal range is broad;
internal components change frequently;
they invoke more external resources;
they contain several decision paths;
they may contain several specialized subjects.

The boundary of a general-purpose subject may therefore depend more on:

governance continuity;
evidence continuity;
core learning structure;
goal-selection mechanisms;

than on fixed components.

## 30. Platforms and Subject Boundaries

Platforms are a special and important structure in subject-boundary analysis.

A platform may claim to be merely:

an information intermediary;
a technical tool;
a market entrance;
a matching service.

But if it actually controls:

task access;
price;
ranking;
qualification;
evaluation;
data;
customer relationships;
penalties;
exit;

it may itself be the stronger functional subject, while individuals or organizations on the platform may function as its components.

Analysis must therefore go beyond the platform’s legal description.

It must examine the real:

decision boundary;
information boundary;
return boundary;
liability boundary;
exit control.

## 31. AI Systems and Subject Boundaries

Whether an AI system constitutes an independent subject cannot be determined only by its intelligence level.

Analysis must ask whether it:

has a stable function;
can form choices;
can reject tasks;
controls resources;
maintains continuous evidence;
forms costs and reservation conditions;
receives returns and bears losses;
possesses a liability structure;
can continue evolving;
remains fully controlled by an external subject.

A highly intelligent but fully invoked AI may still be only a component.

A less intelligent system with stable function, resources, pricing, and liability may possess stronger subjecthood.

## 32. Natural Persons and Functional-Subject Boundaries

The personal boundary of a natural person must be distinguished from the boundary of a functional subject.

Joining a functional subject does not give the subject unlimited control over:

the body;
privacy;
time;
identity;
complete historical records;
all capabilities;
life after exit;
relationships with other subjects.

A functional subject may invoke only the capabilities covered by explicit authorization.

The following boundaries must be preserved:

consent boundary;
refusal boundary;
time boundary;
data boundary;
capability-authorization boundary;
return boundary;
liability boundary;
exit boundary.

## 33. Cost Basis of Boundary Formation

Subject boundaries often form through a comparison between:

C_internal_boundary(A) = Cost of maintaining internal relationships, rules, and closure inside subject A

C_external_exchange(A) = Cost of keeping the relevant relationships outside subject A and realizing them through repeated exchange

When:

C_internal_boundary(A) < C_external_exchange(A)

the relevant relationships are more likely to be internalized.

When:

C_internal_boundary(A) > C_external_exchange(A)

the relevant functions are more likely to be outsourced, separated, or temporarily invoked.

This comparison is still affected by:

power;
monopoly;
law;
path dependence;
information asymmetry;
exit barriers.

## 34. Boundary Stability

Subject-boundary stability may be represented conceptually as:

B_stability(A) = f(F_s, D_s, R_s, E_s, L_s, G_s, X_s, C_s)

Where:

F_s: stability of the functional boundary;
D_s: stability of the decision boundary;
R_s: stability of resource relationships;
E_s: continuity of evidence;
L_s: continuity of liability;
G_s: continuity of governance;
X_s: stability of external recognition;
C_s: cost advantage over alternative boundaries.

This is a conceptual model, not a completed mathematical equation.

## 35. Subject Division

A subject may divide when:

internal subsystems form independent closure;
subsystems can price independently;
subsystems possess independent resources;
subsystems maintain independent evidence;
subsystems can independently bear liability;
internal coordination cost exceeds post-separation exchange cost;
goals remain in long-term conflict;
governance cannot maintain several functions together.

After division, the following must be handled:

data;
knowledge;
resources;
returns;
liability;
historical evidence;
external contracts;
natural-person rights.

## 36. Subject Merger

Several subjects may merge because of:

capability complementarity;
resource sharing;
liability integration;
reduction of exchange cost;
stronger robustness;
unified external interfaces;
network effects;
shared risk bearing;
power expansion.

Merger does not necessarily increase social value.

It may also produce:

monopoly;
concentration of power;
difficult exit;
unclear liability;
reduced diversity;
reduced innovation.

## 37. Subject Absorption

A subject may preserve some function while losing:

independent pricing;
task selection;
customer relationships;
data control;
evidence portability;
exit rights;
liability autonomy.

At that point, it may degrade from a subject into a component of another subject.

Absorption does not require a formal legal merger.

Platform control and infrastructure control may also create factual absorption.

## 38. Boundary Failure

A subject boundary may fail because:

the core function disappears;
decision closure is lost;
resource relationships collapse;
evidence continuity breaks;
no one bears liability;
governance collapses;
external actors no longer recognize the structure as a whole;
internal components fully disperse;
another subject completely absorbs it;
the goal space changes fundamentally.

The legal name may continue after functional-boundary failure.

Therefore:

Formal existence ≠ Functional existence

## 39. Preliminary Questions for Identifying Subject Boundaries

When identifying a candidate subject boundary, ask:

Which collection continuously provides the core function?
Which components cannot be replaced at low cost?
Who makes the key decisions?
Who can accept or reject tasks?
Who controls critical resources?
Who forms prices and reservation conditions?
Who preserves performance evidence?
Who receives returns?
Who bears losses?
Who bears liability?
Who can modify rules?
Who can stop the system?
Who can decide division, merger, or exit?
With whom do external actors actually interact?
What remains continuous after internal components change?
Is there a hidden external controller?
Does the legal boundary match the actual functional boundary?
Do natural-person rights remain outside the subject’s control?

## 41. Current Conclusion

A functional-subject boundary is not determined by biological identity, legal registration, or organizational name alone.

It is a dynamic structure jointly formed by:

Function
→ decision
→ resources
→ information
→ capabilities
→ exchange
→ preference and price
→ evidence
→ returns and losses
→ liability
→ governance
→ temporal continuity
→ external identification

Different boundaries may partially coincide or diverge substantially.

Subject identification should therefore not ask only:

Who nominally owns this system?

It should also ask:

Who actually maintains the function, makes decisions, controls resources, forms prices, preserves evidence, receives returns, bears losses, accepts liability, and remains continuous after components change?

The core of functional-subject boundary analysis is to identify:

The scope within which a stable functional collection forms sufficient relational closure to support independent adaptation, exchange, learning, governance, and liability bearing.
