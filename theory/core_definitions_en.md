## 1. Purpose of This Document

This document defines the foundational objects, relationships, and boundaries of Functional Subject Theory.

The central question of the theory is not to determine in advance which organizational form should dominate future society.

It is to identify:

What kinds of stable functional systems can become effective subjects in sustained production, exchange, governance, and responsibility relationships?

The document therefore distinguishes among:

natural persons;
legal subjects;
functional components;
functional sets;
functional systems;
functional subjects;
capabilities;
tasks;
resources;
preferences;
reservation conditions;
contracts;
evidence;
liability;
governance;
subject boundaries.

## 2. Natural Person

A natural person is a human individual with biological continuity, subjective experience, and fundamental rights.

In Functional Subject Theory, a natural person is first a:

subject of rights;
subject of experience;
subject of consent;
subject of refusal;
subject of appeal;
subject of personal welfare;
basic controller of their own time and capabilities.

A natural person may participate in one or more functional subjects, but their fundamental rights must not depend entirely on their performance within any functional subject.

Therefore:

Natural person ≠ Functional subject

A natural person may be a complete functional subject or only one component of a functional subject.

One natural person may provide different capabilities to multiple functional subjects at the same time.

## 3. Legal Subject

A legal subject is an entity recognized by an existing legal system as capable of holding rights, bearing duties, or participating in legal relationships.

Legal subjects may include:

natural persons;
companies;
nonprofit organizations;
government agencies;
partnerships;
other legally established organizations.

A legal subject and a functional subject may overlap, but they do not have to coincide.

Examples include:

a company may be both a legal subject and a functional subject;
a cross-company human–AI production network may already function as a stable functional subject without being an independent legal subject;
a registered shell company may be a legal subject without being a real functional subject;
a natural person may be a legal subject while different parts of their capabilities participate in several functional subjects.

Therefore:

Legal recognition does not automatically prove the existence of a functional subject.

The existence of a functional subject does not automatically create legal-subject status.

## 4. Function

A function is a persistent, externally identifiable effect or result produced by a system.

A function may include:

producing a class of products;
providing a class of services;
making a class of judgments;
maintaining infrastructure;
performing governance tasks;
coordinating resources;
bearing certain risks;
preserving and updating knowledge;
verifying results.

A function is not identical to the result of a single task.

A function should possess some degree of:

repeatability;
identifiability;
continuity of output;
evaluability;
real value to external subjects.

## 5. Functional Component

A functional component is one of the smallest identifiable units that contributes capability, resources, processes, or constraints to a function.

A functional component may be:

a natural person;
one capability of a natural person;
an AI model;
an AI agent;
a machine;
a dataset;
a program;
an interface;
a rule;
a knowledge base;
an organizational department;
an audit process;
a liability-bearing institution.

A functional component does not necessarily constitute a subject.

A component may:

lack independent decision capacity;
lack external exchange capacity;
bear no independent liability;
have no independent price structure;
function only within a larger system.

Therefore:

Functional component ≠ Functional subject

## 6. Functional Set

A functional set is a combination of multiple components organized around a function.

A functional set may be:

a temporary combination;
a repeated collaboration structure;
a stable production combination;
a functional network that has not yet become an independent subject.

A functional set only means:

These components jointly perform a function.

It does not automatically imply:

independent decision-making;
independent pricing;
independent resource control;
independent liability;
long-term preferences;
external contracting capacity.

Therefore:

Functional set ≠ Functional subject

## 7. Functional System

A functional system is a functional set with persistent internal relationships and operating processes.

It includes at least:

inputs;
processing;
outputs;
component relationships;
operating rules;
some degree of continuity.

A functional system may persist for a long time while remaining fully dependent on a larger subject.

Examples include:

an internal testing system within a firm;
a medical-imaging diagnosis process within a hospital;
a recommendation system within a platform;
a quality-control module within a factory.

These systems may be highly stable without possessing independent subjecthood.

A functional system must form further closure before becoming a functional subject.

## 8. Functional Subject

A functional subject is a functional system that can continuously provide a stable function and form relative closure in decision-making, resources, exchange, evidence, and liability.

A candidate functional subject will usually need:

a stable function;
external identifiability;
a relatively independent decision loop;
control or coordination of necessary resources;
its own cost structure;
its own preference or choice structure;
the ability to accept or reject tasks;
the ability to price, negotiate, or participate in allocation;
continuity of performance evidence;
a structure for receiving returns;
a structure for bearing losses;
a liability structure;
internal governance;
the capacity to evolve, merge, divide, or exit.

A functional subject does not need to be completely independent in all dimensions.

Subjecthood may exist by degree.

Possible categories include:

weak functional subject;
candidate functional subject;
stable functional subject;
highly independent functional subject.

## 9. Subject Closure

Subject closure means that a functional system forms a relatively complete internal cycle in several key dimensions instead of having every critical decision directly controlled by an external subject.

Subject closure includes at least the following.

Functional Closure

The system can continuously receive inputs and produce stable outputs.

Decision Closure

The system can make some degree of independent choice according to tasks, environment, and internal state.

Resource Closure

The system can invoke, coordinate, or reallocate key resources required for task completion.

Exchange Closure

The system can form prices, accept conditions, reject conditions, or participate in other coordination mechanisms.

Evidence Closure

The system can preserve records of performance, failure, capability change, and resource use.

Liability Closure

The system can bear outcomes and allocate responsibility among internal components or external guarantee structures.

A functional subject does not need complete autonomy in all closures.

However, greater closure usually indicates stronger subject independence.

## 10. Capability

A capability is the real possibility that a component or subject can perform a type of operation or produce a type of result under specified conditions.

A capability description must include at least:

what can be done;
under what conditions;
what resources are required;
expected quality;
probability of failure;
expected completion time;
repeatability;
evidence source.

A capability is not merely an abstract label.

For example, “can program” is not a sufficient capability definition.

A more complete capability description should include:

problem types;
tools used;
manageable scale;
delivery standards;
completion time;
risk;
historical evidence.

A capability may belong to:

a natural person;
an AI system;
a machine;
a functional component;
a functional subject.

## 11. Capability Provision

Capability provision is the process in which a capability holder agrees to contribute a capability to a task or functional subject under specified conditions.

Capability provision must be separated from capability possession.

Therefore:

Possessing a capability ≠ Willingness to provide the capability

Willingness to provide a capability ≠ Willingness to provide it under any conditions

Capability provision may depend on:

return;
time;
risk;
autonomy;
opportunity cost;
physical burden;
ethical constraints;
learning value;
long-term return;
impact on other subject relationships.

## 12. Task

A task is a goal structure that must be completed under specified conditions and produce an identifiable result.

A task should include at least:

objective;
inputs;
output requirements;
time constraints;
resource constraints;
risks;
verification method;
liability conditions;
acceptable cost;
failure-handling method.

Tasks may be:

one-time tasks;
repeated tasks;
long-term tasks;
public tasks;
market tasks;
governance tasks;
maintenance tasks;
exploratory tasks.

A task does not automatically create a subject.

However, repeated and structurally similar tasks requiring stable capability combinations may promote functional-subject formation.

## 13. Resource

A resource is an object required to complete a task or sustain subject operation and subject to constraints on use.

Resources may include:

capital;
time;
computing power;
data;
equipment;
energy;
land;
software;
knowledge;
interpersonal coordination;
legal authority;
decision rights;
brands;
trust;
risk capacity;
public infrastructure.

Resource control does not only mean ownership.

It may also include:

usage rights;
dispatch rights;
access rights;
income rights;
temporary authorization;
exclusion rights;
sharing rights;
revocation rights.

## 14. Preference

A preference is a relatively stable tendency expressed by a subject when choosing among actions, tasks, or contracts.

Preferences may include:

return preference;
risk preference;
time preference;
autonomy preference;
learning preference;
reputation preference;
stability preference;
social-value preference;
long-term development preference;
exit preference.

The preference of a functional subject does not necessarily equal the preference of one internal natural person.

It may arise from:

internal governance rules;
component preferences;
contractual obligations;
historical development path;
resource constraints;
risk structure;
long-term functional objectives.

## 15. Reservation Condition

A reservation condition is the minimum set of conditions a subject requires before accepting a task or exchange relationship.

Reservation conditions may include:

minimum monetary return;
maximum acceptable risk;
maximum time commitment;
minimum autonomy;
resource guarantees;
liability limits;
intellectual-property conditions;
data-use restrictions;
exit conditions;
future revenue rights;
learning opportunities;
reputation effects.

A reservation condition is not necessarily a single price.

It is usually a multidimensional set of conditions.

A subject forms effective supply only when task conditions satisfy its reservation conditions.

## 16. Price

Price is the expression of conditions used to coordinate resources, capabilities, risks, time, and rights in an exchange relationship.

Price may take the form of:

money;
resources;
authority;
revenue rights;
risk bearing;
time flexibility;
reputation;
data access;
learning opportunities;
decision participation;
long-term cooperation rights.

Therefore, price in Functional Subject Theory is usually a multidimensional contract condition rather than a single number.

A subject’s price curve represents how much capability it is willing to provide or how many tasks it is willing to undertake under different:

task conditions;
risks;
resource requirements;
opportunity sacrifices;
long-term values.

## 17. Contract

A contract is an identifiable agreement among subjects concerning tasks, resources, rights, returns, risks, and liability.

A contract may be:

a legal contract;
a platform rule;
an automatically executed protocol;
an internal organizational agreement;
a community rule;
a public authorization;
an informal but verifiable collaboration agreement.

A contract should specify at least:

participating subjects;
task;
inputs;
outputs;
time;
return;
resources;
risk;
verification;
liability;
exit;
dispute resolution.

## 18. Evidence

Evidence is an information object used to demonstrate capability, performance, resource use, decision process, or attribution of liability.

Evidence may include:

historical delivery records;
test results;
audit records;
operation logs;
transaction records;
version histories;
sensor data;
third-party verification;
user feedback;
failure records;
decision grounds;
responsibility signatures.

Evidence should possess some degree of:

traceability;
verifiability;
temporal continuity;
source identification;
tamper resistance;
scope specification.

Evidence is not identical to a single score.

Functional Subject Theory rejects reducing complex capability and liability to one unexplained total score.

## 19. Performance

Performance is the process in which a subject completes a task and delivers results according to agreed conditions.

Performance evaluation should distinguish at least:

whether the task was completed;
output quality;
time performance;
resource use;
risk control;
external impact;
liability fulfillment;
compliance with contract constraints.

Performance outcomes may affect:

subject reputation;
cost structure;
future reservation conditions;
resource access;
subject boundaries;
internal governance;
continued subject stability.

## 20. Return

A return is positive value obtained by a subject or internal component through a task, exchange, or long-term participation.

Returns may include:

money;
resources;
authority;
knowledge;
data;
reputation;
learning;
market position;
future revenue rights;
subject stability;
realization of social value.

Returns do not have to occur immediately.

The theory should distinguish among:

immediate returns;
delayed returns;
risk returns;
long-term capability accumulation;
internal surplus value.

## 21. Loss

A loss is negative value produced by task failure, resource consumption, forgone opportunity, liability, or external harm.

Losses may include:

financial loss;
time loss;
resource depletion;
reputational loss;
loss of authority;
legal liability;
loss of future opportunity;
capability degradation;
harm to other subjects.

If a functional subject can receive returns but cannot bear any losses or liability, its subjecthood is incomplete.

## 22. Liability

Liability is the obligation of a subject or component to explain, correct, compensate for, or accept restrictions resulting from actions, decisions, and outcomes.

Liability may include:

operational liability;
management liability;
approval liability;
design liability;
data liability;
model liability;
supervision liability;
compensation liability;
exit liability;
long-term-impact liability.

Liability must be able to pass through complex subject structures.

When a functional subject consists of natural persons, AI systems, machines, and organizations, liability cannot simply be assigned to “the system.”

It must be clarified:

who defined the objective;
who supplied the data;
who controlled the resources;
who approved the decision;
who could stop the system;
who received the return;
who should bear the loss;
who bears final external liability.

## 23. Liability Structure

A liability structure is the relationship system through which liability is identified, allocated, tracked, and enforced inside and outside a functional subject.

A liability structure should include:

source of liability;
object of liability;
scope of liability;
triggering conditions;
liability evidence;
liability limits;
liability-transmission relationships;
compensation mechanisms;
appeal mechanisms;
remediation mechanisms.

A liability structure is an important condition of functional-subject formation.

A functional set without a liability structure is closer to a tool or informal network than to a complete subject.

## 24. Governance

Governance is the process through which rules are created, executed, modified, and audited within a subject or among multiple subjects.

Governance may include:

objective formation;
authority allocation;
resource allocation;
conflict resolution;
member admission;
member exit;
risk management;
rule modification;
auditing;
forced termination;
return distribution;
liability handling.

Governance mechanisms may include:

monocentric governance;
polycentric governance;
market coordination;
voting;
contracts;
hierarchical management;
algorithmic coordination;
community self-governance;
hybrid mechanisms.

Functional Subject Theory does not assume one universal governance mechanism.

## 25. Subject Boundary

A subject boundary is the relational interface separating a subject’s internal structure from its external environment.

A subject boundary is not a single line.

It includes at least:

functional boundary;
decision boundary;
resource boundary;
information boundary;
return boundary;
risk boundary;
contract boundary;
evidence boundary;
liability boundary;
governance boundary.

These boundaries may not fully coincide.

For example:

a subject may use external resources;
a subject may share data;
a subject may rely on an external legal subject for liability;
a subject may cross multiple firms;
one firm may contain multiple functional subjects.

A subject boundary should therefore be understood as a multilayer relational structure rather than one legal-registration boundary.

## 26. Subject Independence

Subject independence is the capacity of a functional system to maintain its function and choices without complete dependence on direct external control.

Subject independence may be reflected in whether the system can:

reject tasks;
form prices;
reallocate resources;
modify internal structure;
choose collaborators;
preserve performance records;
receive returns and bear losses;
exit independently;
maintain functional continuity.

Subject independence is not an absolute state.

It exists by degree.

## 27. Subject Continuity

Subject continuity is the capacity of a functional subject to preserve identifiable function, evidence, and liability continuity across multiple tasks and time periods.

Continuity does not require all internal components to remain unchanged.

A functional subject may continue to exist after changes in members, tools, or resources, as long as its core functional, decision, evidence, and liability structures remain continuous.

Therefore:

Component continuity ≠ Subject continuity

Continuity of legal name ≠ Continuity of functional subject

## 28. Subject Evolution

Subject evolution is the process through which a functional subject changes its structure in response to changes in environment, technology, resources, and tasks.

Subject evolution may include:

absorbing new components;
removing old components;
expanding capabilities;
narrowing functions;
changing internal governance;
changing price curves;
changing liability structures;
merging with other subjects;
dividing into multiple subjects;
becoming a component of another subject.

## 29. Subject Exit

Subject exit is the process in which a functional subject ceases independent operation and terminates or transfers its functions, resources, contracts, and liabilities.

Exit may be triggered by:

disappearance of functional demand;
excessive internal coordination cost;
inability to continue performance;
unsustainable liability risk;
emergence of a superior alternative subject;
voluntary departure of internal components;
governance failure;
external forced termination;
voluntary dissolution.

Exit must address:

unfinished tasks;
remaining resources;
returns;
losses;
data;
knowledge;
liabilities;
rights of internal components;
external affected subjects.

## 30. Minimum Questions for Subject Recognition

Functional Subject Theory does not yet define one fixed recognition formula.

However, a candidate subject should answer at least the following questions:

Does it continuously provide an identifiable function?
Does it have a relatively stable internal structure?
Can it form its own choices?
Can it accept or reject tasks?
Does it possess an identifiable cost structure?
Can it participate in exchange or allocation?
Can it preserve performance evidence?
Can it receive returns and bear losses?
Does it have a traceable liability structure?
Can it preserve continuity when internal components change?
Can it evolve, divide, merge, or exit?
Do external subjects recognize and interact with it as a whole?

The more conditions are satisfied, the stronger the subjecthood.

## 31. Foundational Relationships in the Theory

Foundational relationships in Functional Subject Theory include:

natural persons provide capabilities;
components form sets;
sets form systems;
systems form candidate subjects;
subjects accept tasks;
subjects invoke resources;
subjects state reservation conditions;
subjects form contracts;
subjects perform;
performance produces evidence;
evidence updates subject capability;
outcomes produce returns and losses;
liability structures allocate liability;
governance rules modify subject structure;
subjects evolve or exit.

These relationships will form the basis for later formalization and simulation.

## 32. Current Boundary of the Definitions

The current definitions remain part of a theory draft.

The following issues are not yet fully determined:

whether subject formation requires all closures;
minimum strength of different closures;
whether AI can independently form preferences;
mapping between legal subjects and functional subjects;
distribution of internal surplus value;
final boundary of liability transmission;
measurement of subject continuity;
resource and liability conflicts among overlapping subjects;
distinction between public functional subjects and market functional subjects;
whether functional subjects can possess non-transferable rights.

These issues will be developed in later documents.
